## LinkedIn article

### The Privacy Review Belongs Before the AI Pilot, Not After It

A harmless-looking AI demonstration can change character as soon as a business connects real customer, employee, prospect, patient, financial, or operational information.

The question is no longer whether the tool can produce an impressive output.

The question is whether the organization can explain and control the collection, use, disclosure, retention, accuracy, access, and protection of the information involved.

The Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada advises organizations using generative AI to address legal authority, meaningful consent where applicable, transparency, explainability, safeguards, limits on sharing personal or confidential information, and privacy by design.

These are not questions to postpone until the pilot succeeds.

This article is educational, not legal advice. Applicable requirements depend on the organization, jurisdiction, sector, information, individuals affected, and proposed activity.

### A demonstration can hide the real privacy problem

A demonstration often uses fictional or sanitized information. An operational workflow usually does not.

It may read messages, summarize records, classify requests, recommend actions, create documents, update systems, or communicate with people.

Each connection can change what information is collected, why it is used, who receives it, how long it remains available, and what consequences follow from an error.

The 2026 joint investigation by Canadian privacy regulators into early ChatGPT models identified concerns involving overcollection, valid consent and transparency, factual accuracy involving personal information, access and correction, deletion, and accountability.

That investigation concerned the provider's practices. It also gives organizations a useful warning against treating privacy as a vendor checkbox.

### Ten questions before real information enters AI

#### 1. What exact business purpose requires AI?

Name the workflow, problem, intended result, and why AI is necessary or proportionate.

#### 2. What information will enter or be created?

List fields, documents, prompts, recordings, images, system data, outputs, and inferences. Identify personal, sensitive, confidential, privileged, regulated, or children's information.

#### 3. What is the authority for each use?

Document the purpose and legal authority for collection, use, and disclosure. If relying on consent, determine whether a person can reasonably understand the nature, purpose, and consequences.

#### 4. Can the same result use less information?

Remove unnecessary fields. Prefer anonymous, de-identified, synthetic, or fictional information when it is sufficient.

#### 5. Where does the information go?

Identify providers, hosting and processing locations, subprocessors, integrations, logs, support access, training settings, retention, deletion, and export paths.

#### 6. What may the provider do?

Review contracts and settings for training, secondary uses, disclosure, security, notification, deletion, audit, portability, subcontracting, and changes to terms.

#### 7. Who can access or act on the information?

Apply the minimum access required. Define roles, permissions, prohibited actions, approvals, logs, and prompt access removal.

#### 8. What will affected people be told?

Explain when AI is used, what information is involved, why, what the system does, important risks, available choices, and how to challenge an important outcome.

#### 9. How will accuracy, security, and human review work?

Test inaccurate personal information, prompt injection, unintended disclosure, excessive output, weak authentication, misuse, and vendor failure. Name decisions that require human review.

#### 10. Who owns the workflow after launch?

Assign responsibility for monitoring, complaints, access or correction requests, incidents, vendor changes, deletion, suspension, and review.

### Canadian hosting is not the same as Canadian compliance

Keeping information in Canada may matter to risk, expectations, contracts, or sector requirements.

It does not establish that the complete workflow complies with applicable privacy law.

The business still needs an appropriate purpose, authority, collection limits, safeguards, retention rules, transparency, accountability, and processes for individual rights.

It must also understand where information travels through integrations, logs, backups, support systems, subprocessors, and model services.

### A privacy policy is not an operating control

Updating a privacy policy may be necessary. It does not configure access, prevent unnecessary data from entering a prompt, restrict provider training, test unintended disclosure, correct inaccurate information, delete retained data, or respond to an incident.

The controls live inside the workflow: approved tools, information rules, role-based access, contract settings, testing, logs, review thresholds, retention schedules, complaint handling, incident response, and an accountable owner.

### The direct answer

If the organization cannot explain the purpose, information, authority, minimum necessary data, processing path, controls, access, transparency, testing, human decisions, retention, and accountable owner, it should not place real information into the pilot.

It can continue testing with fictional or appropriately de-identified information while the questions are resolved.

The objective is not to stop useful AI.

It is to prevent a convenient demonstration from becoming an uncontrolled information practice.

### FAQs

#### Is generative AI compliant with PIPEDA?

There is no universal answer for every tool and workflow. Compliance depends on the organization, applicable law, purpose, information, authority, consent where required, provider terms, safeguards, transparency, retention, individual rights, and actual operation.

#### Can employees paste customer information into an AI tool?

Not merely because the tool is available. The organization must determine whether the use is authorized, necessary, appropriately protected, and covered by approved terms, settings, and policy.

#### Does Canadian hosting make an AI workflow PIPEDA-compliant?

No. Hosting location is one consideration. It does not establish the purpose, authority, collection limits, safeguards, transparency, retention, access rights, accuracy, accountability, or proper operation of the workflow.

#### Is consent always required?

Consent is a central principle, but privacy laws contain specific exceptions and different regimes may apply. Document the legal authority and obtain qualified advice where the answer is uncertain or consequences are significant.

#### What should a business ask an AI vendor?

Ask about processing locations, subprocessors, training, secondary use, retention, deletion, security, encryption, access, logs, breach notification, audit evidence, portability, support access, contract changes, and what happens when the service ends.

#### Is updating the privacy policy enough?

No. A privacy policy is an explanation. The workflow still needs data limits, access controls, provider settings, testing, human review, retention, complaints, incident response, and an owner.
